## ABSTRACT (149 words — 150 한도 내)

Across 93 countries from 2000 to 2022, we show that civil liberties repression—a measurable signature of institutional failure—responds to institutional resilience more than 2.5 times as strongly as it responds to social stress itself. We propose a path-weighted framework in which the social cost of any admissible societal trajectory is given by an action functional over a four-dimensional state space (stress, institutional elasticity, future expectation, policy friction), and the relative probability of each path is computed by Boltzmann-style weighting. Pooled cross-national OLS yields α(stress) = 0.63, β(elasticity) = 1.67, and η(expectation) = 0.73 (all 95% CIs exclude zero; R² = 0.77; N = 2,136). The framework reframes a long-standing debate: stress matters, but where institutions hold, stress is metabolized into reform; where institutions fail, the same stress is metabolized into repression. We outline five falsifiable predictions and a research program in anticipatory social science.

## SIGNIFICANCE STATEMENT (50 words — 일부 NHB 포맷 요구)

Why do some governments repress civil liberties more than others? Cross-national evidence from 93 countries shows that institutional resilience matters 2.5 times more than social stress. The result reframes a stress-centric debate (structural-demographic theory) and outlines a path-weighted framework that compares the social cost of multiple available trajectories.

## COVER LETTER

```
Dear Editor,

We submit for your consideration our Perspective titled "Where Institutions
Fail, Stress Becomes Repression: A Path-Weighted Framework for Anticipating
Societal Trajectories."

The Perspective addresses a question of immediate interest to readers of
Nature Human Behaviour: which societies translate structural stress into
civil liberties repression, and which absorb the same stress through
institutional reform? Across a panel of 93 countries from 2000 to 2022
(2,136 country-year observations), we find that institutional resilience
predicts repression more than 2.5 times as strongly as social stress itself.
This finding reframes a long-standing comparative-politics debate that has
placed stress (in particular, Turchin's structural-demographic theory) at
the causal center—a framework whose three core predictions a recent empirical
test (Georgescu, PLOS ONE, 2023) found unsupported by industrialized-society
data.

The contribution is fourfold. (1) We propose a path-weighted action
functional that compares the social cost of multiple admissible trajectories
(reform, revolution, dissolution, status-quo), rather than focusing on the
timing of a single transition as critical-transition theory does. (2) We
apply the path-integral formalism, previously developed only for illustrative
economic models (Gosselin et al., 2017, 2018), to politically and socially
measured outcomes for the first time. (3) We test the framework empirically
using V-Dem and World Bank indicators, with results robust under fixed
effects, four alternative outcome measures, and 1,000 bootstrap resamples.
(4) We pre-register five falsifiable predictions, including a prospective
forecast for South Korea 2026–2030.

The Perspective is written in an explicitly empirical mode—what the
mathematics economically describes, not what it metaphysically governs.
We delimit its scope to the post-1989 liberal international order, when
nation-states operate within a comparable institutional template, and we
note in §6 that pre-1989 periods (the long nineteenth century, the age of
total war, the bipolar Cold War order) require theoretically distinct formal
models.

We expect the Perspective to be of interest to the journal's broad
interdisciplinary readership: to political scientists working on regime
transition; to complexity scientists working on social tipping points; to
economists working on institutions; and to physicists and applied
mathematicians interested in well-grounded social applications of
statistical mechanics.

A companion full Article, currently in preparation, extends the panel to
1960–2022, incorporates three Korean natural experiments (1997 financial
crisis, 2016–17 presidential impeachment, 2020–24 demographic shock) using
interrupted time series, synthetic control, and difference-in-differences
identification, and tests the three remaining falsifiable predictions
through pre-registered analyses.

We confirm that this manuscript is not under consideration elsewhere, that
all authors approve submission, that there are no conflicts of interest,
and that the V-Dem data are publicly available (Coppedge et al., 2024) and
the World Bank data are publicly available through the World Bank API.
Replication code and the assembled panel will be deposited at Zenodo upon
acceptance.
```
